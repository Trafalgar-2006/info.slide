Computer Vision & AI

The Reality Gap is just vibes, until it isn't

January 2026 · 6 min read

When you're generating synthetic training data, everything looks great on paper. Thousands of perfectly labelled images, exact 6-DOF pose annotations, no motion blur, no sensor noise. Your validation loss is beautiful. You feel like a genius.

Then you point the camera at an actual satellite mockup and the model confidently draws a bounding box around the wall behind it.

The model didn't fail because it was bad. It failed because it learned a world that doesn't exist.

This is the Sim-to-Real gap — the uncomfortable distance between a 3D render and reality. Lighting differences, surface imperfections, lens distortion, sensor noise. Things that feel like minor details when you're in Blender become catastrophic domain shifts at inference time.

What I learned at ISRO LEOS is that you can't shortcut this. You have to be deliberate about making synthetic data dirty. Add random HDR environments. Vary path-tracing parameters. Randomise surface roughness. Make your model see the world slightly broken, because the real world always is.

The GAN-based style transfer roadmap I helped design is essentially a translation layer — take renders, make them look like real imagery before the model ever sees them. It's not perfect. But it narrows the gap enough to matter.

Most things in engineering are like this. The clean version works in theory. Reality asks harder questions.

Open Source & Community

What running a college open source club actually taught me

November 2025 · 4 min read

I became President of MBOSC expecting to spend most of my time talking about code. Turns out I spend most of my time figuring out why no one replied to the WhatsApp message I sent three days ago.

Here's the honest version: community is logistics, not passion. You can have the best workshop idea in the world, but if it's on a Friday evening during exam week, you'll be presenting to four people and a ceiling fan.

Timing, repetition, and showing people why it matters to them — that's the whole job.

The part that surprised me most is how open source contribution is genuinely scary for most people. Not because the code is hard, but because putting your work somewhere public where strangers can judge it takes a specific kind of courage that isn't taught anywhere. A lot of what MBOSC does is just normalising that discomfort.

I've seen someone go from "I don't know enough to contribute" to having their first PR merged, and that shift in confidence is worth every awkward meeting where only three people showed up.

Running a club taught me more about people than about software. Which, honestly, is probably more useful.

On shipping a frog classifier at 2am and calling it research

September 2025 · 3 min read

The frog project started because I was procrastinating on something else and found a dataset of field recordings from the Western Ghats. One thing led to another and at 2am I had a working MFCC pipeline that could identify species with 85% accuracy.

Was it rigorous? Not particularly. Did I have a research question before I started? Absolutely not. Did I learn more about audio signal processing that night than in the entire previous semester? Yes.

The best projects start before you're ready. That's not recklessness — that's the only way curiosity works.

I think a lot about how many projects never start because the person is waiting until they know enough. But knowing enough is a moving target. You always know more after you build the thing than before.

The messy 2am version is the foundation. Clean it up later. Or don't — sometimes the messy version teaches you everything you needed.

The frog classifier lives on my GitHub, has zero stars, and I am completely fine with that.

Open Source & Community

Why open source is the best thing that happened to my learning

August 2025 · 5 min read

University teaches you to write code that satisfies requirements. Open source teaches you to read code that was written by someone who thought very differently from you, made choices you wouldn't make, and left comments that raise more questions than they answer.

That discomfort is the education.

When I started contributing, I'd spend an hour understanding one function. Now I can trace unfamiliar codebases in minutes — not because I got smarter, but because I've been lost in enough of them to have a map of how people get lost.

Reading code you didn't write — with no one to explain it — is the fastest way to develop engineering judgment.

There's also something about the culture. Open source at its best is radically generous. People you'll never meet spent their evenings writing documentation so you wouldn't be confused. That creates an obligation — when you know something, you document it. You answer the issue. You write the test.

Knowledge isn't really yours until you've given it away. That's a lesson I keep coming back to, in code and outside of it.
